# Crossroads of Duty

Xavier Reeves had been a sheriff for thirty-five years in the small town of Meridian, Arizona. His weathered face told stories of countless arrests, shootouts, and the slow grind of justice in a place where the law was sometimes as harsh as the desert that surrounded it. At sixty-two, he was just months away from retirement—a fact he'd been reminding himself of daily as he transported Miles Harrington, a twenty-six-year-old prisoner, to the county courthouse in Phoenix.

Miles wasn't like the other criminals Xavier had encountered throughout his career. The young man had been arrested for stealing medical supplies from the Meridian pharmacy, but the circumstances were unusual. Miles's mother was battling cancer, and their insurance had denied coverage for her pain medication. Desperate to ease her suffering, Miles had broken into the pharmacy after hours. He'd left an IOU note and taken only what his mother needed—nothing more.

"We're lost, aren't we?" Miles asked, his voice cutting through the silence as their transport vehicle sputtered to a final stop on a desolate stretch of back road. They had taken a shortcut through the desert to avoid a reported accident on the main highway.

Xavier wiped sweat from his brow. "Engine's dead. Radio too." He checked his cell phone. "No service out here."

The two men exited the vehicle, the midday sun beating down mercilessly. Xavier assessed their situation: they were at least twenty miles from the nearest town, with only a single canteen of water between them. The old lawman knew the desert well enough to understand the gravity of their predicament.

"We need to start walking," Xavier said, removing Miles's handcuffs. "No point in these now. We're in this together."

Miles rubbed his wrists. "I have to make it to Phoenix by tomorrow morning."

"Court date isn't until the afternoon," Xavier replied.

"It's not about court." Miles hesitated. "I'm supposed to meet someone. It's important."

Xavier didn't press for details. They began walking, rationing small sips of water as the sun crawled across the sky. By late afternoon, Xavier was struggling. His pace slowed, and his breathing became labored.

"Sheriff, you need to rest," Miles insisted, noticing the older man's deteriorating condition.

"Can't stop," Xavier mumbled, but his legs gave out beneath him. He collapsed onto the hot sand.

Miles knelt beside him, feeling for a pulse. It was there, but weak. Xavier was unconscious, his skin burning with fever. Miles looked ahead at the empty road stretching toward Phoenix, then back at the fallen sheriff.

He could leave now. The thought crossed his mind briefly. He could take the water and make it to Phoenix in time for his meeting. No one would blame him for saving himself.

But Miles couldn't abandon the man. He hoisted Xavier onto his shoulders in a fireman's carry and continued walking, the burden slowing his pace considerably. Each step was a struggle against exhaustion and the knowledge that his own important engagement was slipping away.

---

Three years earlier, Miles had been a different person. Fresh out of college with a business degree, he'd returned to Meridian with dreams of starting his own company. He'd reconnected with his high school sweetheart, Molly Chen, who had stayed in town working at her family's restaurant while completing online courses in graphic design.

Their reunion had been magical—until Martin Drake returned to town. Martin had been Miles's best friend since childhood, but he'd left for the military after graduation. Now he was back, decorated and confident, and he too had history with Molly.

The three friends spent time together, but tension grew as both men's feelings for Molly became apparent. Martin was everything Miles wasn't—decisive, physically imposing, and financially stable with his military pension. Miles, meanwhile, was still struggling to launch his business and care for his increasingly ill mother.

One evening, after too many drinks at the local bar, Miles and Martin had a heated argument about Molly.

"You've been gone for years," Miles said. "You can't just come back and expect everything to be the same."

"And you can't expect her to wait around while you figure out your life," Martin countered.

Their mutual friend Ethan Powell, who had been quietly nursing his own feelings for Molly, intervened. "This is ridiculous. Why don't you both just ask her to choose? End this once and for all."

The alcohol and frustration led to a rash agreement. They would all meet at Molly's apartment the next day at noon and let her decide whom she wanted to be with.

But Miles never made it to that meeting. That morning, his mother had a severe reaction to her medication, and he spent the day in the emergency room. By the time he returned home and checked his phone, he had missed calls from both Martin and Ethan, but none from Molly.

When he finally reached Martin, his friend's voice was cold. "She chose Ethan. Said she couldn't deal with either of our drama anymore."

Miles was stunned. Ethan had never even been part of the equation in his mind. He tried calling Molly, but she wouldn't answer. Two weeks later, he heard they were engaged.

---

As the sun began to set, Miles spotted a gas station in the distance. With renewed determination, he adjusted Xavier's weight on his shoulders and pressed forward. By the time they reached the station, Miles was nearly crawling.

The attendant called an ambulance, which arrived quickly and transported Xavier to the nearest hospital. Miles went along, refusing to leave the sheriff's side until he knew he would be alright.

"You saved his life," the doctor told Miles after Xavier was stabilized. "Another hour in that heat, and he wouldn't have made it."

Miles sat by Xavier's bed until the older man regained consciousness. When Xavier's eyes finally opened, they were clear and alert.

"You could have run," Xavier said weakly.

"Couldn't leave you there," Miles replied simply.

Xavier studied him for a long moment. "Your meeting in Phoenix. What was it?"

Miles checked his watch. It was nearly midnight. "Doesn't matter now. I missed it."

"Tell me anyway," Xavier insisted.

Miles sighed. "Three years ago, I was supposed to meet the woman I loved and two other guys who were interested in her. We were going to let her choose between us. I never made it because my mom got sick. She ended up with one of them—Ethan. But I heard through a friend that they're divorced now, and she's been looking for me."

Xavier's eyebrows rose. "And she was meeting you tomorrow?"

"Today, actually. This morning." Miles shook his head. "She probably thinks I stood her up again."

Xavier reached for the phone beside his bed. "Call her. Explain what happened."

"It's too late."

"Son, I've been married forty years. If there's one thing I've learned, it's that it's never too late when it comes to love."

---

Molly Chen had made a terrible mistake marrying Ethan Powell. She'd done it partly out of spite when Miles didn't show up for their meeting, and partly because Ethan had seemed safe and stable—unlike Miles with his entrepreneurial dreams or Martin with his military career that could take him away at any moment.

But the safety had quickly turned to stagnation. Ethan was controlling, jealous of her friendships, and dismissive of her graphic design aspirations. After two miserable years, she filed for divorce.

During the proceedings, she learned from Martin that Miles had missed their meeting because of his mother's medical emergency. The revelation shattered her assumptions and filled her with regret. After her divorce was finalized, she began searching for Miles, eventually discovering he had moved to Phoenix to expand his now-successful business creating eco-friendly packaging materials.

She had arranged to meet him at a café in Phoenix, hoping for a second chance. When he didn't show, she waited for hours, her hope gradually fading to disappointment.

Her phone rang as she was leaving the café. Unknown number.

"Hello?"

"Molly? It's Miles."

Her heart skipped. "Miles? Where are you? I waited all day."

"I'm at Sunrise Hospital in Westridge. I was bringing a sick man here when I should have been meeting you." He explained the situation with Xavier, his voice tired but determined.

"You haven't changed," Molly said softly. "Still putting others first."

"I'm sorry I missed our meeting. Again."

"Don't be. That's who you are, Miles. It's one of the reasons I've been looking for you." She paused. "I made a mistake three years ago."

"We both did," Miles replied. "But maybe we can start over?"

"I'd like that," Molly said. "I can be there in an hour."

---

Six months later, Xavier stood in his dress uniform at Miles and Molly's wedding. The charges against Miles had been dropped after Xavier testified about his character and the circumstances of the pharmacy incident. Miles's business was thriving, with Molly's designs now featured on all their packaging. His mother's cancer was in remission, thanks to a clinical trial Miles had found and fought to get her into.

During the reception, Xavier raised his glass for a toast. "To Miles and Molly. Sometimes life puts us at crossroads where duty to others conflicts with duty to ourselves. The choices we make in those moments reveal our true character. Miles chose to help a stranger when it would have been easier to walk away. That decision cost him something important, but it also led him back to what he truly needed." He smiled at the couple. "May you both always find your way back to each other, no matter what deserts you have to cross."

As Miles embraced his bride, he reflected on the journey that had brought them together again. The desert ordeal had taught him that sometimes the right path isn't the easiest one, and that wisdom often comes at a high price. But standing there with Molly in his arms, surrounded by friends and family, including a now-supportive Martin, he knew that every step had been worth it.
